Victoria Falls

What Is Victoria Falls?

Victoria Falls, located on the Zambezi River between Zambia and Zimbabwe, is one of the world’s largest waterfalls, with a height of 108 meters and an average flow of 1,088 cubic meters per second. Named by David Livingstone in 1855 after Queen Victoria, its local name, Mosi-oa-Tunya ("the smoke that thunders"), captures its awe-inspiring power and mist, mesmerizing visitors as of May 2025.

Natural Environment

Part of the Mosi-oa-Tunya National Park, spanning 66 square kilometers, Victoria Falls hosts diverse wildlife like elephants, giraffes, and numerous bird species. The falls’ spray sustains unique riverine forests and wetlands, creating vibrant ecosystems—recent 2025 ecological surveys emphasize their role in regional biodiversity amidst climate pressures.

Visitor Activities

Helicopter rides offer panoramic views of the falls’ grandeur, while white-water rafting on the Zambezi provides thrilling adventures through its rapids. Cultural experiences include the Livingstone Museum in Zambia, detailing regional history, and the Victoria Falls Cultural Centre in Zimbabwe, showcasing local traditions, enriching visits in May 2025.

Scientific and Economic Significance

The falls support research into biodiversity, climate change, and river ecology, with 2025 studies focusing on wetland conservation. Economically, the Kariba Dam downstream generates hydropower for Zambia and Zimbabwe, with plans for new plants aiming to meet growing energy demands while balancing environmental impacts.

Challenges and Conservation

Climate change, deforestation, and land-use shifts threaten the falls’ ecosystems, reducing water flow—2025 data shows a 10% flow decrease since 2000. Conservation initiatives promote sustainable tourism and protected areas, ensuring Victoria Falls remains a vital natural and cultural treasure for future generations.
