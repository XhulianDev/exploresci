Second Balkan War

What Was the Second Balkan War?

The Second Balkan War (June–July 1913) was a brief but intense conflict among the former allies of the Balkan League—Bulgaria, Serbia, Greece, and Montenegro—following their victory over the Ottoman Empire in the First Balkan War. Disputes over the division of territorial gains, particularly in Macedonia, led Bulgaria to attack Serbia and Greece on June 29, 1913. The war ended with Bulgaria’s defeat, as Romania and the Ottoman Empire also joined against it, resulting in significant territorial losses for Bulgaria under the Treaty of Bucharest (August 1913).

This conflict deepened Balkan rivalries, setting the stage for World War I.

Origins and Territorial Disputes

The First Balkan War concluded with the Treaty of London (May 1913), granting the Balkan League control over most Ottoman territories west of the Enos–Midia line, including Macedonia. However, a pre-war agreement between Serbia and Bulgaria (March 1912) to divide Macedonia—with Bulgaria taking the south and Serbia the north—unraveled when Serbia captured more territory than agreed, reaching the Bitola–Gevgelija line. Greece’s occupation of Thessaloniki further complicated matters. Serbia, forced by the Great Powers to cede northern Albania, refused to relinquish its Macedonian gains, while Bulgaria insisted on enforcing the original partition, leading to irreconcilable tensions.

These disputes turned allies into adversaries within weeks of their shared victory.

Escalation and Alliances

Anticipating Bulgarian aggression, Serbia and Greece signed a secret defensive alliance on June 1, 1913, securing their mutual border in Macedonia and pledging support against Bulgaria or Austria-Hungary. Bulgaria, under Prime Minister Geshov, initially sought a diplomatic solution by agreeing to Greek control over southern Macedonia, but his dismissal halted negotiations. Tensions also arose with Romania over Bulgaria’s refusal to cede Silistra, resolved through Russian arbitration in the Protocol of St. Petersburg (May 1913). However, Russia’s failure to mediate the Serbian-Bulgarian dispute led Bulgaria to lose faith in Russian support, isolating it further.

Bulgaria’s diplomatic missteps left it vulnerable to a multi-front war.

The War and Its Outcome

On June 29, 1913, Bulgaria launched a surprise attack on Serbian and Greek positions in Macedonia, hoping for a quick victory. However, Serbia and Greece, now allied, repelled the offensive. Romania joined the conflict, seeking Silistra, while the Ottoman Empire re-entered to reclaim Eastern Thrace. Outnumbered, Bulgaria faced rapid defeats, losing key territories within weeks. The Treaty of Bucharest (August 10, 1913) stripped Bulgaria of most of its Macedonian gains, ceding land to Serbia, Greece, and Romania, while the Ottomans regained Eastern Thrace under the Treaty of Constantinople.

The war’s brevity belied its devastating impact on Bulgaria and regional stability.

Legacy and Historical Impact

The Second Balkan War entrenched divisions in the Balkans, fueling nationalist resentments that contributed to World War I. Bulgaria’s territorial losses fostered a desire for revenge, aligning it with the Central Powers in 1915. The war also solidified Serbia and Greece as regional powers, while the reassertion of Ottoman control in Thrace delayed their decline in Europe. Culturally, the conflict inspired literature and songs reflecting national pride and loss, while its lessons on alliance fragility influenced Balkan politics for decades.

This war remains a cautionary tale of the perils of unchecked territorial ambition.
