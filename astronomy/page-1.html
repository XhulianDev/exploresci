The Big Bang Theory

What Is the Big Bang Theory?

The Big Bang theory posits the universe began 13.8 billion years ago from a singularity, expanding rapidly via inflation into the cosmos we know today, supported by evidence as of May 2025.

Historical Development

Proposed by Georges Lemaître in the 1920s as the "primeval atom," the theory gained traction in the 1960s with the discovery of cosmic microwave background radiation by Penzias and Wilson, affirming its validity.

Key Evidence

Cosmic microwave background radiation, uniform at 2.7 Kelvin, and the abundance of hydrogen and helium align with Big Bang predictions. Galaxy distribution further supports the theory’s early uniformity, as noted in 2025 studies.

Cosmological Insights

The theory reveals dark matter (27% of mass) and dark energy (68%), driving accelerated expansion. It also traces the early universe’s atom formation, shaping our understanding of its evolution by May 2025.

Challenges and Future

Unresolved issues include matter-antimatter asymmetry and dark matter detection. Future research may clarify the universe’s fate—expansion, Big Crunch, or Big Rip—as explored in 2025 cosmological models.
