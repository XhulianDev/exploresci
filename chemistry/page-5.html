Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies the heat energy absorbed or released during chemical reactions. This energy change provides deep insight into how and why reactions occur, as well as their potential applications in everyday life and industry.

What is Thermochemistry?

Thermochemistry examines the transfer of heat between a system and its surroundings during a chemical or physical change. It helps chemists understand not just what products are formed, but also how energy is involved in the process.

Enthalpy (ΔH)

Enthalpy is a measure of the total heat content of a system at constant pressure. It is denoted by the symbol H, and the change in enthalpy during a reaction is represented as ΔH. This change tells us whether the system has absorbed or released heat:

  • ΔH < 0 → The reaction is exothermic (heat is released)
  • ΔH > 0 → The reaction is endothermic (heat is absorbed)

Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Exothermic Reactions

In an exothermic reaction, the system releases heat to its surroundings. These reactions feel warm or hot and include combustion, neutralization, and many oxidation processes. Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O  ΔH = -890 kJ/mol

Endothermic Reactions

Endothermic reactions absorb heat from the surroundings, making the environment feel cooler. They include photosynthesis, decomposition of calcium carbonate, and melting ice. Example: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂  ΔH = +178 kJ/mol

The First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics, also known as the law of conservation of energy, states that energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be transformed from one form to another.

In thermochemistry, this means that the total energy change in a system must equal the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system:

ΔU = q - w

  • ΔU = change in internal energy
  • q = heat exchanged
  • w = work done by the system

This principle helps chemists calculate how much energy is stored or released during chemical transformations and ensures energy accounting is always accurate.

Applications of Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry plays a vital role in fields like engineering, environmental science, material science, and even medicine. It helps optimize fuel usage, design efficient engines, monitor environmental changes, and develop new energy sources.

Conclusion

Thermochemistry bridges the gap between chemical reactions and energy. By understanding enthalpy changes and applying the laws of thermodynamics, we gain powerful tools to predict and control energy flow in chemical systems, from industrial reactors to biological cells.
