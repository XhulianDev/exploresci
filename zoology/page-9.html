Wolf

What Are Wolves?

Wolves (Canis lupus), the largest Canidae family members, are intelligent, social predators. Males weigh 70–110 pounds, females 50–85 pounds, with broad heads and powerful jaws. Once widespread, gray wolves in the U.S. now number around 6,000, a recovery from near extinction by May 2025.

Social Structure

Wolves live in packs led by an alpha pair, including their offspring and related members. They communicate via howls, growls, and scent marking, fostering pack loyalty. In 2025, studies show packs adapting to fragmented habitats, strengthening social bonds for survival.

Ecological Role

As apex predators, wolves regulate prey like deer and elk, benefiting plant communities and balancing predator populations like coyotes. Their predation prevents overgrazing—2025 research highlights their role in restoring ecological balance in Yellowstone after reintroduction.

Diet and Hunting

Primarily carnivorous, wolves hunt elk, deer, and smaller mammals, often scavenging or eating plants. They use teamwork to take down prey, with howls coordinating hunts. Recent 2025 observations note increased scavenging in areas with reduced prey due to habitat loss.

Conservation Challenges

Wolves face habitat loss, human conflict, and diseases like parvovirus. Protected under the U.S. Endangered Species Act, their population grows, but persecution persists. May 2025 efforts focus on habitat restoration, reintroduction, and education to reduce conflicts and ensure their survival.
