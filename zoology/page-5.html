Komodo Dragon

What Is the Komodo Dragon?

The Komodo dragon (Varanus komodoensis), native to Indonesian islands like Komodo and Rinca, is the world’s largest lizard, reaching 10 feet and over 150 pounds. An apex predator, it hunts deer, pigs, and buffalo, dominating its ecosystem as of May 2025.

Physical Characteristics

Komodo dragons feature mottled gray-brown skin with osteoderms, powerful legs, and a muscular tail used for balance and defense. Their jaws, armed with serrated teeth, deliver venomous bites causing rapid blood loss and paralysis, an adaptation enhancing their active hunting strategy.

Hunting and Adaptations

With a unique respiratory system—long trachea and enlarged lungs—Komodo dragons chase prey efficiently. Their venom, packed with toxic proteins, ensures quick kills. They strike with precision, often ambushing larger animals, as observed in 2025 field studies on Rinca Island.

Habitat and Threats

Komodo dragons inhabit a limited range, making them vulnerable to habitat loss from logging, agriculture, and natural disasters like earthquakes. Poaching for illegal trade further threatens their survival, with 2025 estimates showing only 4,000–5,000 individuals remaining in the wild.

Conservation Efforts

Classified as vulnerable by the IUCN, Komodo dragons benefit from protected areas like Komodo National Park, home to 2,500 individuals. Captive breeding programs, such as the Komodo Survival Program in 2025, aim to boost genetic diversity and support reintroduction, ensuring their long-term survival.
