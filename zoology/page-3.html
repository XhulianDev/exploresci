Tiger

What Are Tigers?

Tigers (Panthera tigris), the largest cats in the Felidae family, encompass five endangered subspecies: Bengal, Indochinese, Siberian, Malayan, and Sumatran. Renowned for their orange coats with black stripes, they are apex predators, shaping ecosystems across Asia despite a 40% population decline over the past decade as of May 2025.

Physical Characteristics

Tigers boast powerful jaws, sharp teeth, and a muscular build, ideal for hunting large prey. Their iconic striped coats provide camouflage, though variations like the white Bengal tiger exist. Skilled swimmers, they navigate rivers to hunt or claim territories, showcasing adaptability in diverse environments.

Behavior and Ecology

Primarily solitary, tigers hunt with stealth, stalking prey like deer and boars for hours before striking. Males may form coalitions, while females fiercely guard cubs. Requiring vast territories—up to hundreds of square miles—they thrive in forests, grasslands, and wetlands, maintaining ecological balance as apex predators.

Habitat and Distribution

Tigers inhabit tropical forests, grasslands, and wetlands across Asia, but habitat loss has shrunk their range. Once widespread, they now face fragmentation due to agriculture and urbanization—2025 surveys estimate only 3,900 wild tigers remain, underscoring the urgent need for habitat preservation.

Conservation Challenges

Listed as endangered by the IUCN, tigers suffer from habitat loss and poaching for fur and bones used in traditional medicine. May 2025 initiatives, including protected areas, anti-poaching patrols, and community programs, aim to reduce human-tiger conflict and ensure their survival through sustainable practices.
